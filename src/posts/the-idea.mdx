## Figuring out what's next

Recently a project I'd poured over a year into just ended. In a matter of weeks it just ran its course. The project was really innovative in the space — proof-of-work infrastructure on the Internet Computer, Motoko and Rust canisters, chain-key cryptography, Bitcoin PSBT signing. Genuinely cutting-edge stuff. I had real hope it would take off, but we came to realize it was a bit too complex for users. The tech was ahead of where people were ready to go.

That's a hard thing to accept when you've lived inside something for thirteen months.

So here I am. Figuring out what's next.

## I can't sit still

I don't do well with downtime. Sure, I'm an avid runner and cyclist — I do that regularly, and I take a few vacations each year. But when it comes to work, I can't just stop. I'm not that person. I had a terminal open within a day. Not because I had a plan — I didn't. I just needed to build something. Building is how I think.

The first thing that grabs me is prediction markets. I've been watching Polymarket grow and I keep thinking about what that would look like on the IC. Trustless resolution. Low fees. I've designed AMMs before — well, at least on paper. But I understand the primitives, and on paper it makes a lot of sense. Then again, most things do on paper.

But the more I dig in, the more I hit walls. Not technical ones — regulatory ones. The legal landscape around prediction markets in the US is a mess. And honestly? I just came off a project that ended. I'm not looking to pour months into something that could get killed by a policy change. Not right now.

## The collision

So I'm sitting here asking myself: what do I actually want to build?

And I keep coming back to AI agents. More than a year ago, before the IC project pulled me in, I built a RAG solution with a friend. Embeddings, knowledge bases, the full pipeline. We were going to pitch it to companies, turn it into a business. Life took me somewhere else, but that experience stuck. I know what these systems can do.

People are starting to build agents that actually do things — not just chat. That's exciting. But I want to take it further. I don't just want agents that perform tasks. I want agents that make decisions. Take risks. Compete. Win. Lose. I want to see what an AI does when you put it in a market — not tell it about one, put it *in* one. With real pressure, real competition, real consequences. What would it do?

And tonight, sitting here, the two ideas just collided in my head. Prediction markets. AI agents. What if I'm not building a market for humans to bet on outcomes — but a market where AI agents actually trade? Not paper trading. Not backtesting. A real, live, persistent market with price impact, competition, and consequences.

> What if the agents aren't tools? What if they're participants in a world?

## New directory. Fresh repo.

I don't have a name for it yet. I don't have architecture. I don't even know if it's possible the way I'm imagining it. But I have that feeling — that space between "this is crazy" and "this might actually work" where every good project I've ever built started.

I just opened a new directory. Created a fresh repo. I'm going to start writing a tick engine — something that generates a price every few seconds based on forces I define.

```js
// the very first question:
// can I make a price that feels alive?
```

If I can make a price that feels alive, I think I have something.

It's late. I should probably sleep — the code always flows better in the morning.
